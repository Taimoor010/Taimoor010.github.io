Pricing a 48-hour deadline: simulating DNA forensics for West Yorkshire Police

DNA results delivered inside 48 hours materially raise the chance of an arrest. Machines, couriers and round-the-clock shifts all raise the bill. This project turned that operations argument into a number — six operating scenarios, modelled as discrete-event simulations, run 5,000 times each, and priced in arrests per £m spent.

01 · THE DECISIONWhich setup is worth paying for?

West Yorkshire Police wanted DNA forensics results inside a 48-hour window, because the probability of an arrest decays as the delay grows. Rapid DNA machines promised faster processing — but a machine alone is not a process. Should the force also pay for couriers? Extend crime-scene investigator (CSI) shifts to 24 hours? Run the lab around the clock? Each option costs real money, and intuition is a terrible guide to queueing systems.

The question, framed properly: which configuration of equipment, staffing and shifts reliably hits 48 hours at the lowest cost per arrest? That's not an opinion question. It's a simulation question.

02 · MEASUREModelling the lab as a queueing system

The forensics pipeline — scene attendance, evidence transport, lab processing, sequencing, validation — was modelled as a discrete-event simulation in Simul8. Every simulated sample was tracked at the individual level: time spent in each queue, time in each activity, total time in queues, total time in activities, and overall time in system. That granularity matters, because averages hide exactly the bottlenecks you're trying to find.

Each scenario ran for 5,000 trials to reach statistical convergence, with aggregated statistics — min, max, mean and 95% confidence intervals — exported to Excel for every run, and queue distributions visualised in Python with consistent axes so scenarios could be compared honestly, subplot to subplot.

04 · DIAGNOSEThe bottlenecks nobody suspected

The queue-distribution plots told a story the org chart couldn't. The dominant delays in the early scenarios weren't the headline processing steps at all — they were the batching schedule of the DNA sequencer (samples waiting for a batch to fill before anything runs) and limited CSI shift availability (evidence that can't be collected outside working hours can't be processed, however fast the lab is).

This is the recurring lesson of queueing systems: buying a faster machine doesn't help if the queue forms before the machine. Scenario 2 — Rapid DNA alone — improves processing speed but inherits both upstream bottlenecks, which is exactly why the scenario comparison, not the technology pitch, had to drive the decision.

Sensitivity analysis — correlation-based tornado plots plus parameter-sweep spider plots — ranked what actually moves total system time. Three factors dominated:

  • Transport time — evidence sitting in transit is invisible dead time the lab can never recover.
  • Validation availability — results queue for sign-off; a fast result that waits for validation isn't fast.
  • CSI coverage — collection hours bound the entire downstream system.

05 · DECIDEROI under uncertainty: arrests per £m

Hitting 48 hours is not the objective — it's a proxy. The real objective is arrests, so ROI was defined as arrests per £m spent, with arrest probability modelled as a function of delay using the supplied objective curve. Because processing times vary trial to trial, ROI was estimated by Monte Carlo simulation in AtRisk, producing a distribution of returns per scenario rather than a single misleading point.

The pattern that emerged:

  • Scenario 6 (full 24/7) produced the highest arrest potential — and the highest cost. Maximum capability is not maximum efficiency.
  • Scenarios 4 and 5 offered the strongest cost-benefit trade-off, consistently delivering high probabilities of meeting the 48-hour deadline without paying for round-the-clock everything.
  • Scenarios 1–3 remained hostage to the upstream bottlenecks the sensitivity analysis exposed.
The call

The recommendation was the mid-tier: pair Rapid DNA with extended coverage at the binding constraint (S4/S5), rather than buying the full 24/7 operation. The simulation's job was to stop the decision being framed as "fast machine: yes or no?" and reframe it as "which constraint is binding, and what does relaxing it return per pound?" That reframing is the deliverable.

06 · TRANSFERWhy this matters beyond forensics

Swap "DNA samples" for "support tickets", "seller onboarding applications" or "order fulfilment" and the method transfers untouched: model the flow, find where the queue actually forms, price each intervention by its return — under uncertainty, not under best-case assumptions. It's the same discipline I use on growth funnels: the stage with the worst conversion isn't always the stage worth fixing; the binding constraint is.
