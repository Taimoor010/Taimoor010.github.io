How much could I lose tomorrow? Portfolio risk, three ways

A portfolio-risk pipeline that starts with Markowitz construction and then asks the uncomfortable question properly: how much downside do different model assumptions imply? Normal VaR says 7.60%. GARCH says 7.74%. Monte Carlo says 10.56%. The gap is the point.

01 · THE DECISIONRisk is a model choice before it is a number

"How much could this portfolio lose?" sounds like one question. In practice it is three questions hiding inside one: how do you construct the portfolio, how do you model volatility, and how much tail uncertainty do you accept? I built the workflow to make those assumptions visible instead of letting a single clean number pretend to be truth.

The project used six Bloomberg daily price series across equities, a bank, a bond ETF and a currency-converted index, then added a synthetic risk-free savings asset for comparison. The output is not an investment recommendation; it is a repeatable risk framework that turns modelling assumptions into decision-ready numbers.

02 · MEASURECleaning assets into comparable returns

Raw prices were aligned, FX-adjusted where needed, resampled to month-end observations and converted into monthly returns. That gave the portfolio construction stage inputs it could actually trust: expected return, volatility and a covariance matrix across assets.

The correlation matrix mattered because diversification is the whole point of portfolio construction. High-return assets dominated the eventual max-Sharpe allocation, but lower-correlation assets still had a job: reducing portfolio-level volatility rather than winning on standalone return.

03 · CONSTRUCTFind the portfolio, then interrogate it

Using Monte Carlo simulation, 10,000 random portfolios were generated across the risky assets. Each portfolio's expected return, volatility and Sharpe ratio were calculated from the monthly return series. The selected portfolio was the one with the highest Sharpe ratio, giving the risk stage a clear object to test.

The optimal allocation concentrated in the highest-return assets, with smaller weights assigned where diversification improved the portfolio. That is exactly the trade-off Markowitz is designed to expose: not which asset is exciting, but which combination pays the most return per unit of risk.

04 · RISKThree answers to the same loss question

The 1-month 95% VaR was estimated three ways. The Normal model assumes constant volatility and normally distributed returns. GARCH(1,1) allows volatility to move through time, responding to clustering around turbulent periods. Monte Carlo samples a wider distribution of possible outcomes, producing the most conservative estimate.

The call

The final answer should not be "the VaR is 7.6%" or "the VaR is 10.6%". The decision-ready answer is: under simple distribution assumptions, the downside threshold is around 7.6%; under simulation, it is materially wider. A manager can now decide how conservative the risk control needs to be.

05 · DECIDEThe assumption is the risk

The project made one lesson very explicit: portfolio risk is not just in the assets, it is in the modelling lens. If the decision is capital allocation, a 3-point gap in downside estimate is not academic. It changes position sizing, cash buffers and whether a portfolio feels acceptable under stress.

GARCH adds realism around volatility clustering. Monte Carlo adds a more pessimistic view of tail behaviour. Normal VaR is useful because it is fast and explainable, but it should not be the only answer when the cost of underestimating loss is high.

06 · TRANSFERWhy this belongs in a decision-systems portfolio

The same discipline transfers outside finance. Build the object you are deciding on, test it under multiple assumptions, and show the spread between answers. In growth analytics, that might be attribution models. In operations, it might be demand forecasts. In risk, it is VaR. The method is the same: surface the assumption before it silently makes the decision for you.
