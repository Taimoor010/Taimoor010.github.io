CarGauge: pricing 58,000 used cars in your browser

An end-to-end machine learning system that estimates used-car asking prices in Pakistan — from a scraper that respects the marketplace, through a cleaning layer that turned listings into vehicles, to a LightGBM model that ships as pure JavaScript. No backend. No API. Just a model, deployed.

01 · THE QUESTIONWhat is this car actually worth?

Pakistan's largest auto marketplace, PakWheels, hosts tens of thousands of live listings — and no public pricing intelligence on top of them. Buyers anchor on whatever the seller typed. Sellers guess. I wanted to know whether listing data alone could answer the pricing question with calibrated confidence, and whether the answer could run as a real product anyone can use.

One framing decision shaped everything downstream: the model predicts advertised asking price, not transaction price, because asking price is what the data actually contains. Pretending otherwise would have baked an invisible error into every estimate. Instead, the product handles the gap explicitly — more on that in section 07.

02 · MEASUREGetting the data without getting blocked

The obvious scraping approach — visit every listing's detail page — is slow, brittle, and the fastest route to a rate limit. So the Playwright pipeline was built around search-result cards instead: title, exact numeric price, listing ID, city, year, mileage, fuel, engine, transmission, badges and photo count, all extracted from the search pages themselves.

Two fields that matter for price — colour and registration — don't appear on cards. The fix: crawl filtered search pages (e.g. "white cars only") and join the labels back to the main dataset by listing ID. Hidden attributes recovered without touching a single detail page. Raw values were preserved untouched throughout; every cleaned field was written separately, so the dataset stays auditable.

03 · CLEANThe real work: making listings mean vehicles

The model algorithm was never the hard part. The hard part was that listings describe text, not vehicles. A sample of the battles:

  • Honda Civic generations. A 2016 Civic can be two very different cars. Generation was separated from family and variant using deterministic year–engine–variant rules; ambiguous transition-year rows were excluded rather than guessed.
  • Missing zeros. A car listed at 850,000 that should read 8,500,000 destroys a regression. High-confidence missing-zero cases were corrected in a separate model field — the raw price column was never touched.
  • Mileage placeholders. Annualised mileage was sanity-checked against make/model/year cohorts; extreme values were imputed into a dedicated mileage_model_km field.
  • Rare vehicles. Variants with at least 5 rows train exactly; rarer trims fall back to model-level. Make/model groups with ≤20 rows were excluded from the public scope entirely.
  • Leakage bans. Listing IDs, URLs, price cohorts and any target-derived field were explicitly banned from the feature schema.

04 · EXPLOREWhat the market data says

Exploration before modelling confirmed the structure the model would need. Log price correlates strongly with engine size (r ≈ 0.69) and model year (0.64); vehicle age is its mirror (−0.64); mileage matters (−0.33) but is noisy because vehicle class dominates it. Depreciation is steep and nonlinear — which is why the target is log price and the models are trees. And the market is concentrated: three Japanese makes account for over three quarters of supported listings.

05 · MODELChoosing a model — and rejecting a better one

Ten model families were benchmarked on an 80/20 price-stratified holdout plus 6-fold stratified cross-validation, all on log price: gradient boosting in four flavours, random forest, extra trees, CatBoost, ridge, and MLP challengers. The gradient-boosted family clustered tightly at the top; the linear baseline trailed by 7 points of MAPE — a clean measure of how nonlinear this market is.

The call

A richer internal feature schema — scraping-quality flags, support counts — scored slightly better in one benchmark (7.17% MAPE). I rejected it. Those fields describe my dataset, not the user's car, and a public estimator can only use what a real person can type into a form. The support signals moved into confidence scoring instead, where they belong. Offline metrics are not the product.

06 · DIAGNOSEWhere the model struggles

A headline MAPE hides more than it reveals, so the holdout was cut by segment. The errors were not random — they concentrated exactly where economics says they should: thin support, cheap cars where small misses become large percentages, and luxury imports where condition and trim dominate price.

07 · DECIDEA price is not enough

The product never shows a lone number. Every estimate ships with a calibrated 90% range (built from holdout residuals; realised coverage 90.01%, median width 33.9% of the prediction) and a confidence label scored from interval width, training support, variant fallback, mileage risk, colour missingness and price band:

Confidence levelHoldout rowsWhat it signals
High9,700Well-supported vehicle, exact variant, tight interval
Medium742Some risk flags — wider range, read it as a band
Low107Thin support or fallback variant; treat as directional
Very Low58The honest answer is "we don't really know"

And because the target is asking price, a separate deterministic post-processing rule estimates an expected deal price by applying a negotiation discount by price band. Keeping that rule outside the model means sale-price assumptions never contaminate training — and can be updated without retraining anything.

08 · SHIPShipping without a backend

The selected LightGBM model and its metadata export to a static JavaScript bundle. The website loads the bundle, populates its form options from model metadata — users can only select supported make/model/variant combinations — and produces the estimate, deal price, range and confidence label entirely in the browser. Hosting is GitHub Pages on a personal domain. Zero servers, zero latency budget, zero monthly bill.

The call

Static deployment is a deliberate architecture decision, not a shortcut. For an MVP, a backend adds cost, failure modes and maintenance for no user benefit. The trigger points for adding one are written down in the roadmap: model bundles outgrowing the browser, user accounts, or server-side retraining on live snapshots. Until one of those fires, simplest wins.

09 · NEXTWhat I'd build next

The next quality jump isn't a fancier algorithm. It's data operations: archive every weekly scrape as an immutable snapshot, validate schema and drift before cleaning, add time-based validation once multiple snapshots exist, and monitor what users actually ask the live estimator for. EVs join the scope once battery and trim coverage can support them honestly. The model is the easy part to upgrade — the discipline around it is what makes the number trustworthy.
