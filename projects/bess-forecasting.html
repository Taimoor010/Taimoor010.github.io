Store or sell? Forecast-driven battery trading in the UK power market

Wind farms leave money on the table because output is variable and prices are volatile. My MSc dissertation built the full decision system: deep-learning forecasts of wind power and electricity prices, feeding a mixed-integer optimiser that decides — every hour — whether to sell, store, discharge or wait. The uplift is quantified, and so are the caveats.

01 · THE DECISIONEvery hour, four options

A wind producer with a co-located battery faces the same decision every hour: sell the generation now, store it, discharge what's stored, or sit idle. Get it right and you arbitrage the spread between cheap, windy hours and expensive, scarce ones. Get it wrong and you've cycled an expensive battery for nothing — degradation is a real cost, charged per kWh.

The decision is only as good as two forecasts: how much wind power will exist in the next six hours, and what electricity will be worth. So the project is really two forecasting problems welded to one optimisation problem, built on UK-specific data — ERA5 meteorological reanalysis for the weather, ENTSO-E day-ahead market prices for the money, a full year at hourly resolution.

02 · MODELForecasting prices: the hybrid wins

Four architectures — MLP, 1D-CNN, LSTM and a CNN-LSTM hybrid — were trained to predict the next six hours of UK day-ahead prices from an 18-hour lookback, with hyperparameters tuned by Optuna (Bayesian search, up to 100 trials per model). Price signals mix sharp local spikes with longer daily cycles, and the hybrid's structure mirrors that: convolutions catch the spikes, recurrence catches the rhythm.

03 · MODELForecasting wind: the plot twist

Same four architectures, same protocol, different winner. On wind power, the humble MLP beat every sequence model — lowest RMSE (1,285 kW), highest R² (0.7016), best at five of six horizons. With a rich ERA5 feature set, wind prediction behaves less like a sequence problem and more like nonlinear regression, and a well-tuned MLP is exactly that. Meanwhile the LSTM scored the highest Pearson correlation (0.862) — it tracks the shape of the series best even where its magnitudes miss.

The call

No single architecture dominated both tasks — and that's the finding, not a failure. The price task rewarded the hybrid; the wind task rewarded a flexible function approximator; the "best" model even changes with the metric you care about (point accuracy vs shape tracking). A production system should run a portfolio of specialised models per data stream, not crown one architecture and force it everywhere.

04 · OPTIMISEThe optimiser: profit, with physics attached

Forecasts feed a mixed-integer linear programme (PuLP, CBC solver) that maximises revenue over each six-hour window while respecting how batteries actually work:

The degradation term is the quiet hero: it makes the optimiser conservative in flat markets and aggressive in volatile ones, which is exactly the behaviour a real asset manager wants.

05 · RESULTSWhat the decision system earned

Against a baseline that sells all generation immediately, the forecast-driven optimiser averaged £858.85 per six-hour window vs £801.72 — a +7.13% uplift across the dataset. The average hides the texture, which three windows illustrate:

  • Steady spread (+38.5%): prices climb gradually; the optimiser charges early, sells late, and beats the baseline comfortably.
  • Price spike (+109.1%): the forecast sees a spike coming; the battery pre-charges and discharges aggressively into the peak. The baseline, selling as it generates, misses most of it.
  • Spike, but no fuel (+2.4%): a late spike with little wind available — the optimiser anticipates correctly but has almost nothing stored to sell. Forecast quality can't conjure energy.

06 · HONESTYThe limitations, stated plainly

The framework treats point forecasts as if they were certain, so the reported uplift is an upper bound under that assumption — realised gains would be reduced by forecast error. The six-hour horizon can't hold charge for tomorrow's bigger spike. Degradation is modelled as a flat linear cost when real degradation is nonlinear in temperature, depth of discharge and C-rate. And one year of training data meets a market that policy and the generation mix keep reshaping.

The call

I reported the 7.13% explicitly as an idealised ceiling rather than a promised return. An analysis that hides its assumptions gets one good meeting; an analysis that prices them in gets trusted with the next decision. The fix list is concrete: probabilistic forecasts feeding risk-aware (stochastic or robust) optimisation, longer or nested horizons, richer degradation models, rolling retraining.

07 · NEXTWhere this goes

The most interesting extension is economically-driven forecast training: stop optimising RMSE and start optimising the profit the downstream decision actually realises — the forecast exists only to serve the decision, so train it on the decision's objective. Beyond that: multi-market participation (day-ahead, intraday, balancing, ancillary services) and reinforcement-learning dispatch policies that learn the market directly.
